How does the writer use language to describe Sister Brendan?

Posted: 2021-02-25   |   Tags: english, language, paper two, question three

The writer immediately plants doubts about Sister Brendan, using the simile “small hungry blackbird”. The adjective “hungry” is extended to “out for the early worm”, which suggests Phinn sees the headmistress as an eager and dominant figure. Yet this is juxtaposed against the semantic field describing her physique — “tiny”, “little”, “small”, perhaps reminding the reader that he is not dominated by the Sister; that he is the inspector. The writer continues this repetition of contrasting the Sister’s ardent speech with negative imagery immediately after: in the shaking of the hands, hers are described as “cold”, and her eyes “look up” into his after the Sister asking about the writer’s journey. Compare how the writers convey their different attitudes to the two schools

Posted: 2021-02-25   |   Tags: english, language, paper two, question four

In both sources, the surroundings are abysmal, yet the staff share very different attitudes to the school. In Source A, Sister Brendan is eager to show off her fantastic pupils to the inspector, whereas in Source B, the writer dreads school. The writer in Source A shows Sister Brendan’s zeal in the way she moves: she “glided through the school”. Evaluate the environmental impact of extraction of raw materials in Nigeria

Posted: 2021-02-07   |   Tags: environment, geography, nigeria, oil extraction, raw materials

(revisions in bold) Oil extraction can lead to oil spills which are highly damaging to local ecosystems, such as the Bodo oil spills between 2008-2009. Because of the hugely dense deposit of oil (11 million gallons across 20km²), it will take at least 30 years for the area’s ecosystem to regenerate. The damage can be amplified in undersea oil pipelines and offshore oil pumps as special diving equipment or specialised for bigger oil spills, which take a long time to deploy and, therefore, to fix the oil spill.
