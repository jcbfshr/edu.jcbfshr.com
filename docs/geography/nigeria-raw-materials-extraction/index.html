Evaluate the environmental impact of extraction of raw materials in Nigeria

Posted: 2021-02-07   |   Tags: environment, geography, nigeria, oil extraction, raw materials

(revisions in bold) Oil extraction can lead to oil spills which are highly damaging to local ecosystems, such as the Bodo oil spills between 2008-2009. Because of the hugely dense deposit of oil (11 million gallons across 20kmÂ²), it will take at least 30 years for the area’s ecosystem to regenerate.

The damage can be amplified in undersea oil pipelines and offshore oil pumps as special diving equipment or specialised for bigger oil spills, which take a long time to deploy and, therefore, to fix the oil spill.

Nigeria has lost 70-80% of its forests. Trees in forests slow down the velocity of rain which allow it to gently seep into the soil. If the trees are cut down, the water accumulates too quickly and rolls over the land, causing mass flooding and overflowing rivers, which can damage soil as the nutrients are washed away. Waterlogged soil also causes the roots of plants to rot, meaning the plants will die.

Altogether, the extraction of raw materials is highly environmentally damaging to Nigeria. Nigeria has lost a huge proportion of its forests, causing yet more plant damage in a negative feedback loop. Oil extraction will also harm local ecosystems, as it has already done, for as long as the oil is being extracted. The ecosystems never recover quickly, and oil extraction will certainly leave a deep scar on Nigeria’s environment.
